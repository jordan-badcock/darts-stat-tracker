Brick / terracotta — #D85A30Warmth, energy, and sporting culture. Red-orange tones spike competitive drive without pure aggression — the colour of pub signage, dart flight graphics, and worn leather. Deeply resonant with 30–60 male darts players.
Amber — #EF9F27Belonging and reward. Pub lighting, real ale, and trophy metals. In data UI, amber highlights personal bests and league leaders — the warm glow that says "you're winning here."
Pitch background — #120800The pub at night. A near-black with brown undertone (not cold blue-black) evokes a warm, lived-in space rather than a tech product. Separates HQ from generic sports apps.
Harmony: warm analogous + complementBrick → amber forms a tight warm analogous band. Felt green (#1D6B40) is the complement of red, grounding the scheme in the dartboard's own colours. Triadic structure with amber completing the triad.

Emerald to teal — #1D9E75 / #5DCAA5Growth, precision, and forward momentum. Green is the dartboard's playing surface — the palette lives inside the game itself. Unlike warm pub palettes, this reads as a performance tool: sharp and modern.
Near-black — #040A07Dark-mode first suits evening and weekend playing patterns. Emerald metrics glow against near-black the way a lit dartboard does in a dim pub. Preferred by male sports app users aged 25–55.
Mint text — #E0F4ECExtended readability without eye fatigue. Mint on near-black is softer than pure white — the "night vision" quality of F1 timing screens and football analytics overlays.

Deep violet / purple — #7B3FBF / #9B5FDFUnexpected, powerful, and genuinely distinctive. No major darts brand uses purple — immediate visual differentiation. Purple is associated with prestige, royalty, and competition. Against near-black it reads as a premium night-time performance brand.
Warm gold — #C8A84BThe one warm note in an otherwise cool palette — creates maximum visual tension (purple and gold are complementary-adjacent). Used by sports royalty: Lakers, Minnesota Vikings, Fiorentina. Immediately elevates the brand above generic.
Void background — #0A0512A near-black with a violet undertone rather than blue or brown — everything on it vibrates with a slightly electric quality. The palette as a whole evokes the atmosphere of a serious competition night: stage lighting, smoke, focus.
Harmony: analogous purple + split-complement goldViolet and grape (#7B3FBF → #9B5FDF) form a tight analogous band. Gold (#C8A84B) is the split-complementary warm accent. The scheme is bold but controlled — never garish because the base is so dark.

Blackboard / near-black — #141410The scoreboard itself. A warm-tinted near-black (olive undertone, not blue or brown) evokes a real chalk scoreboard in a pub. For the 35–65 male darts demographic, this triggers genuine nostalgia — every league night started with chalk in hand.
Chalk yellow / bone — #D4C88A / #F5F0E0Handwritten scores and tallies. These off-whites mimic real chalk on a dark board — warmer than pure white, they avoid the clinical feel of modern apps. The entire palette is saying: this sport has history, and so do you.
Graphite mid-tone — #8A8060Secondary information, muted stats, and supporting UI elements. This warm grey sits between the background and the chalk foreground — it's the half-erased score, the faded legend. Creates depth without extra colours.
Harmony: monochromatic + single complementFive stops of the same warm neutral (near-black → bone) create a tight monochromatic scheme — supremely restrained and analogue-feeling. Burgundy (#8B2020) is the single complement: the red bull ring, used only for critical stats and alerts.
